Japan's Wild North

Hokkaido is Japan's northernmost and second-largest island, but it's a world away from the temples and traditions of Honshu. It was only fully settled by the Japanese in the late 1800s — before that, it was the homeland of the Ainu, an indigenous people with their own distinct language, culture, and spiritual traditions.

The Meiji government actively colonised Hokkaido from the 1870s, bringing in American agricultural advisors to develop farming. This is why Hokkaido feels different — wider roads, grid-pattern towns, vast farmland, and a frontier spirit unlike anywhere else in Japan.

Did you know? Hokkaido makes up 22% of Japan's total land area but has only 4% of the population. In summer, its rolling green hills and lavender fields look more like Provence than Japan.

Furano & the Lavender Fields

Furano's lavender fields were originally planted in 1958 as a commercial crop for perfume production. When synthetic alternatives took over in the 1970s, the fields were nearly ploughed under. But a single photograph of Farm Tomita published in a national railway calendar saved them — tourists started arriving by the thousands.

Today, Farm Tomita is one of Hokkaido's most iconic sights, with rows of purple lavender set against a backdrop of green mountains. July is peak season, which is exactly when you'll be there. Furano also produces some of Japan's best melons and wine.

Did you know? You can eat lavender ice cream, lavender honey, and even lavender ramune soda at Farm Tomita. The lavender essential oil they produce is considered some of the finest in the world.

Biei's Blue Pond

The Blue Pond in Biei became world-famous almost overnight when Apple used a photo of it as the default wallpaper for Mac OS X Mountain Lion in 2012. Its striking cobalt-blue colour comes from naturally occurring aluminium hydroxide particles in the water, which scatter light in a way that creates the vivid blue.

The pond was actually created by accident in 1988 as part of an erosion control project after a nearby volcanic eruption. Dead birch and larch trees standing in the water add to its otherworldly appearance. The colour is most vivid on clear, calm days.

Did you know? The Blue Pond's colour changes throughout the year and even throughout the day. In summer mornings it's an electric turquoise; by afternoon it shifts to a deeper cobalt. Overcast days actually produce the most vivid blues.
