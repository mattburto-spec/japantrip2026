A Planned City

Sapporo is one of Japan's youngest major cities, founded in 1868 as the administrative centre for Hokkaido's colonisation. American advisor Horace Capron helped design the city on a grid pattern — unusual for Japan but inspired by American city planning.

The city grew rapidly, hosting the 1972 Winter Olympics which put it on the world map. Today it's Japan's fifth-largest city with nearly 2 million people, known for its wide boulevards, excellent food scene, and the famous Sapporo Beer — Japan's oldest beer brand, brewed here since 1877.

Did you know? Sapporo's name comes from the Ainu language: "sat poro pet" meaning "dry, great river." Despite being a modern Japanese city, many place names in Hokkaido preserve the original Ainu language.

Otaru: The Canal Town

Otaru was once Hokkaido's busiest port and financial centre — in the early 1900s it had more banks than almost anywhere in Japan. When the herring fishing industry collapsed in the 1950s, the city declined, but its beautiful stone warehouses and canal were preserved.

Today the canal district is one of Hokkaido's most romantic spots, lined with converted warehouses housing glass workshops, music box shops, and seafood restaurants. Otaru is also famous for its incredibly fresh sushi — the fish comes straight from the port that morning.

Did you know? Otaru's Music Box Museum houses over 25,000 music boxes, making it one of the largest collections in the world. The building itself is a converted 1912 warehouse, and a steam clock at the entrance chimes every 15 minutes.

Beer, Lamb & Chocolate

Sapporo's food scene is unlike the rest of Japan. Genghis Khan (jingisukan) lamb BBQ is the city's signature dish — you grill thin slices of lamb on a domed griddle, a tradition that started when the government encouraged sheep farming in Hokkaido in the early 1900s.

The Sapporo Beer Museum, housed in a gorgeous red-brick 1890 building, tells the story of Japan's oldest beer brand. And Shiroi Koibito Park (the chocolate factory) is home to Hokkaido's most famous souvenir — white chocolate biscuits that sell millions of boxes every year.

Did you know? Hokkaido produces over half of Japan's milk, so dairy here is exceptional. The ice cream, cheesecake, and butter are noticeably richer than in the rest of Japan — locals say it's the cool climate and happy cows.
