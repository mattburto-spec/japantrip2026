The Imperial Capital

Kyoto served as Japan's capital for over a thousand years, from 794 to 1868. The city was modelled on the Chinese Tang Dynasty capital of Chang'an, laid out in a perfect grid that you can still see in the street pattern today.

During World War II, Kyoto was initially on the list of atomic bomb targets, but US Secretary of War Henry Stimson — who had visited the city on his honeymoon — argued passionately to remove it. That decision preserved over 2,000 temples, 17 UNESCO World Heritage sites, and centuries of irreplaceable culture.

Did you know? Kyoto is an anagram of Tokyo. When the capital moved east in 1868, the new city was named "Eastern Capital" (To-kyo), while Kyoto means simply "Capital City."

Fushimi Inari & the Ten Thousand Gates

Fushimi Inari Shrine is dedicated to Inari, the Shinto god of rice, prosperity, and foxes. The shrine's famous tunnel of vermillion torii gates winds 4km up Mount Inari — there are over 10,000 of them, each donated by a Japanese business hoping for good fortune.

The tradition of donating gates dates back centuries, but many of the current ones are modern. A small gate costs around $4,000 AUD, while the largest can cost over $15,000. Each gate is inscribed with the donor's name and date.

Did you know? The fox statues at Inari shrines aren't pets — they're divine messengers. Look closely and you'll see them holding different objects in their mouths: a key (to the rice granary), a jewel (wisdom), or a scroll (knowledge).

Arashiyama & the Bamboo Grove

The Arashiyama bamboo grove has been celebrated in Japanese literature for centuries. The towering stalks of Moso bamboo can grow up to 20 metres tall and reach full height in just 40 to 60 days — you can practically watch them grow.

The grove borders Tenryu-ji, a 14th-century Zen temple with gardens designed by the famous monk Muso Soseki. The area was originally a retreat for Heian-era aristocrats — the "storm mountain" name comes from the cherry blossoms that would blow like a storm in spring.

Did you know? Japan's Ministry of Environment has listed the sound of wind blowing through Arashiyama's bamboo grove as one of the "100 Soundscapes of Japan" worth preserving.

Nara's Ancient Temples

Just a day trip from Kyoto, Nara was actually Japan's capital before Kyoto, from 710 to 784. Todai-ji temple houses the world's largest bronze Buddha — the Daibutsu stands 15 metres tall and weighs 500 tonnes. The wooden hall that houses it is one of the largest timber buildings on Earth.

Nara's 1,200+ free-roaming deer have lived in the city for centuries. In Shinto mythology, a god arrived in Nara riding a white deer, so the animals have been considered divine messengers and protected ever since. They'll bow to you if you bow first — it's a learned trick for getting treats!

Did you know? The Great Buddha was cast in 752 AD and required 2.6 million workers over the course of several years. It used so much copper and gold that it nearly bankrupted the Japanese government at the time.
