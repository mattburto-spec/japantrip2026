A City of Resilience

Hiroshima's name means "Wide Island," and the city was founded in 1589 on the delta of the Ota River. For centuries it was a prosperous castle town and military hub — but everything changed on the morning of 6 August 1945.

The atomic bomb destroyed nearly everything within two kilometres of the blast. Yet Hiroshima chose to rebuild not as a monument to destruction, but as an international symbol of peace. Today it's a vibrant, modern city with a deeply moving mission.

Did you know? The city was expected to be barren for 75 years. Instead, oleander flowers bloomed in the rubble just months later. The oleander became Hiroshima's official flower.

The Peace Memorial

The A-Bomb Dome (Genbaku Dome) is the only structure left standing near the bomb's hypocentre. Originally the Hiroshima Prefectural Industrial Promotion Hall, its skeletal frame has been preserved exactly as it was after the blast.

The surrounding Peace Memorial Park was designed by architect Kenzo Tange in the 1950s. It includes the Memorial Museum, the Children's Peace Monument (decorated with thousands of paper cranes), and the cenotaph that frames the dome at the end of a long reflecting pool.

Did you know? Sadako Sasaki, a young girl who developed leukaemia from the bomb's radiation, tried to fold 1,000 paper cranes — a Japanese symbol of healing. Her story inspired the Children's Peace Monument, and visitors still leave cranes there today.

Miyajima Island

Just a short ferry ride from Hiroshima, Miyajima (officially Itsukushima) has been considered one of Japan's three most scenic views for centuries. The island's iconic floating torii gate was first built in 1168 and has been rebuilt many times since.

The gate stands in the tidal flats so that at high tide it appears to float on the water. The whole island is considered sacred — historically, no births or deaths were permitted here to maintain its purity. Even today, there's no cemetery on the island.

Did you know? The island's friendly deer are considered sacred messengers of the gods in Shinto belief. Unlike the deer in Nara, Miyajima's deer roam completely wild and aren't officially fed — though they'll happily investigate your shopping bags.
