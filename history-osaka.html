Japan's Kitchen

Osaka has been called the "Nation's Kitchen" for over 400 years. In the Edo period, it was the centre of Japan's rice trade — merchants from every province shipped their goods here, and with all that produce came an obsession with food that never left.

The locals even have a word for it: kuidaore, which roughly translates to "eat until you drop." From takoyaki (octopus balls) on every corner to the sizzling okonomiyaki griddles, food isn't just sustenance here — it's identity.

Did you know? Osaka consumes more takoyaki than any other city in Japan — over 5 billion balls per year. Most families own a special takoyaki pan at home.

Osaka Castle & the Unifier

Osaka Castle was built in 1583 by Toyotomi Hideyoshi, the warrior who unified Japan after a century of civil war. At the time it was the largest castle in the country, designed to project power and awe.

The castle has been destroyed and rebuilt several times — most recently in 1931 as a concrete reconstruction. Today it sits inside a beautiful park that's especially lovely in summer, surrounded by moats and stone walls that have survived since the 1600s.

Did you know? The castle's stone walls contain some of the largest stones ever used in Japanese construction. The biggest, called "Octopus Stone," weighs about 130 tonnes.

Dotonbori & Shinsekai

Dotonbori is Osaka's neon-drenched entertainment district, running along a canal that was dug by hand in 1612. The famous Glico Running Man sign has stood here since 1935 and is one of the most photographed spots in all of Japan.

Nearby Shinsekai ("New World") was built in 1912 as a futuristic entertainment district — half inspired by New York, half by Paris. Today it has a wonderfully retro feel, famous for its kushikatsu (deep-fried skewers) and the iconic Tsutenkaku Tower.

Did you know? There's an unwritten rule in Shinsekai's kushikatsu restaurants: never double-dip in the communal sauce. Signs everywhere remind you — it's taken very seriously!
