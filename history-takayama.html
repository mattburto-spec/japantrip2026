Little Kyoto in the Mountains

Takayama sits at 573 metres in the Japanese Alps and has been called "Little Kyoto" because its beautifully preserved old town mirrors the elegance of the former capital. But while Kyoto's beauty comes from temples, Takayama's comes from its perfectly kept merchant houses.

The Sanmachi Suji district dates back to the Edo period (1600s-1800s). Its three narrow streets are lined with dark-timber sake breweries, craft shops, and family businesses that have been running for generations. In summer, the mountains surrounding the town are a lush, deep green.

Did you know? Takayama's carpenters were so skilled that the Imperial court regularly summoned them to build temples and shrines in Kyoto and Nara. They were exempted from paying taxes in exchange for their craftsmanship.

The Takayama Festival

Takayama Matsuri is considered one of Japan's three most beautiful festivals, held every spring and autumn since the 16th century. Elaborate floats called yatai are paraded through the streets — some are over 300 years old and designated as Important Cultural Properties.

The floats feature incredible mechanical puppets (karakuri) that perform dances and tricks using a system of strings and gears designed centuries ago. Even if you're not there during festival time, you can see the floats year-round in the Yatai Kaikan exhibition hall.

Did you know? The mechanical puppets on the festival floats can pour tea, write calligraphy, and even do backflips — all powered by hand-pulled strings. The techniques are family secrets passed down through generations.

Shirakawa-go: A World Apart

An hour's bus ride from Takayama, Shirakawa-go is a UNESCO World Heritage village where families still live in dramatic gassho-zukuri farmhouses. The name means "praying hands" — the steep thatched roofs (up to 60 degrees) are shaped to shed the region's heavy snowfall.

These houses are up to 250 years old, and some are four storeys tall. The upper floors were traditionally used for silkworm cultivation, the steep roof creating natural ventilation. The entire community works together to re-thatch roofs — a tradition called "yui" that reinforces village bonds.

Did you know? Re-thatching a single Shirakawa-go roof takes 200 villagers working together and requires replacing the entire roof in a single day. The community schedules these events years in advance.
